1. Discriptive Statistics

The North Valley Real Estate Data comprises 105 records. The price of these records range from approximately 160,000 to 920,000 US dollars. The average selling price is close to 360,000 US dollars. About 65% of the houses come with a pool (n = 67), and about 75% come with a garage (n = 78).

3. Correlation

Next, this paper wants to explore if there is a correlation between the size of the house and the price, so it first uses a scatter plot to see where the sample data falls.

Then, a loess model (Local Polynomial Regression Fitting) is applied (Figure 4). As Yifai Xie said, taking a specific percentage of data allows us to observe patterns and trends in the data than the usual regression analysis which tends to model the whole data. Real-life patterns are not always (or rarely) a straight line as we are told in textbooks (Xie 2008).

Because the number of observations is less than 1000, the formula used in smoothing function is: \(y ~ x\).

Figure 4: Scatter plot

We determined that house size is highly correlated with price, and then called the plotpairs function in the beginr() package, in order to obtain a regression line and a correlation coefficient of 0.95 (\(p<.01\)) based on the lm model (Figure 5).

Figure 5: Price and Size Correlation

5. Research Questions and Results

RQ1

For RQ1, this paper would like to focus on the Pool and Garage variables: how do these two variables differ in distribution according to the value of the house (price and size)?

Figure 6 shows that houses without pools are more evenly distributed in the scatterplot than houses without garages. In other words, there are still some mid- to high-priced houses without pools, but only low-priced houses don’t have garages, which are a relatively basic amenity in our sample.

Figure 6: Pool & Garage

RQ2

For RQ2, this paper would like to further explore how the characteristics of houses sold by different agents differ?

Result in Figure 7 shows that Marty is better at selling lower-priced, smaller homes than the other four agents.

Figure 7: Agent based analysis

RQ3

For RQ3, this paper would like to explore the correlation between different variables.

Calling the ggstatsplot package (Patil 2021), the result shows that the four variables “Prize, Size, Bedrooms & Baths”, have a high positive correlation with each other (\(p<.05\)). Among all other variables, only the FICO index and the year of the house have a moderate correlation of 0.51 (Figure 8).

Figure 8: Correlations of all variables

6. Conclusion

This paper concludes that:

  1. Although Pool and Garage are two binary variables, by comparing the drop points in scatter plots, we can still derive useful information that Garage is more commonplace than Pool across all facilities in the sample.

  2. The price range of housing transactions is bound to be different for different agents. By including the agents in the comparative analysis, we can find out their advantages in their respective house price ranges and further develop their business strategies in the future.

  3. The variables “Years, Days and FICO index” have almost no correlation, so the primary and secondary variables specified by the teacher are very reasonable.
